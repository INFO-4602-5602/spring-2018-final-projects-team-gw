How safe are vaccines in the United States?


Despite the controversy surrounding the safety of vaccines, most people trust that the CDC and drug manufacturers know what they are doing. Is that a smart choice? The goal of this page is to present (the most objective) numbers (we currently have) regarding injuries or deaths that may be directly attributed to the administering of vaccines in the US.

The Vaccine Adverse Events Reporting System (VAERS) was created to keep track of problematic side effects reported to healthcare providers. This system of record is kept and maintained by the Centers for Disease Control. Each year the VAERS database publishes its dataset documenting complications and deaths which could be the result of immunizations administered in the United States.






Reported Deaths


Some vaccines may be directly responsible for deaths. In 2017, there were a total of 120 deaths recorded after a patient recieved a vaccination. The vaccine with seemingly the highest risk was a combination vaccine, potentially causing 39 deaths. This vaccine consisted of two or more of the following: Diptheria, Tetanus, Pertussis, Polio, Hib, Hepatitis.

Reported Injuries


Many patients don't have reactions as severe as death. Other reactions, which may include migraines, allergies, infections, seizures, dizziness, shock, hepatic failure, acute kidney injury, and a wide variety of other ailments also get reported to VAERS. A total of 38,916 injuries were reported as potentially being a direct result of immunizations in 2017.

Other things to know


There are a couple of points to know about the VAERS numbers. The CDC explicitly states that correlation is not causation. It is unknown whether these reported events are caused by the vaccines, only that they occured AFTER the patient received the vaccine.

Additionally, it should be noted that VAERS is a passive reporting system. This means they rely on patients and doctors to volunteer their accounts/experiences. Because of this, the numbers are likely lower than actual, as not all events will be reported.

For more details on VAERS and its effectiveness and limitations, please refer to:
Shimabukuro TT, Nguyen M, Martin D, DeStefano F. Safety monitoring in the Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System (VAERS). Vaccine. 2015;33(36):4398-4405. doi:10.1016/j.vaccine.2015.07.035.

Source


The data seen here was extracted from the Vaccine Adverse Events Reporting System from the US Department of Health and Human Services.

Please note, the data from VAERS specifically states: "...the inclusion of events in VAERS data does not imply causality."
